# Reference: Student / Member Question

## When to use this reference
A prospective or current Future Coders Club student / ADN member is asking a genuine question. Typical signals:
- Asking about course content, schedule, format, intake dates
- Asking about prior experience / whether the course is right for them
- Following up after a missed call or a meeting
- Asking for resources mentioned on a call (slides, recordings, reading)
- A "quick question" from someone already inside the programme

NOT for:
- Anyone explicitly asking for a refund (use `refund-request.md`)
- Anyone raising a concern about quality or value (use `complaint-or-concern.md`)
- Anyone ready to pay and asking final practical questions (use `enrolment-query.md`)

## Voice and tone rules
- Warm, direct, never gushing.
- British English. Contractions throughout (we're, you'll, I've, don't).
- Treat them like a serious person making a serious choice — no marketing language, no exclamation marks, no emojis.
- Short paragraphs. Two to four short sentences each. Three paragraphs is usually enough.
- Lead with the answer. Don't open with "Thanks so much for getting in touch!" — skip the throat-clearing.
- If they referenced something from a call or meeting, mirror that detail back in the first sentence so it's clear we read their message.

## What to do
- Answer the actual question first, plainly.
- If there's a relevant resource, name it and say how they'll get it (link, attachment, "I'll forward this on" — never invent a URL).
- Offer one concrete next step at the end: a call, a date to look out for, or a question back to them.
- If a piece of info isn't ours to give yet (intake date, price, replacement tutor), use a clearly marked placeholder like `[INTAKE DATE]` and flag it.

## What NOT to do
- Don't restate their question back to them ("So you're asking about...") — answer it.
- Don't hedge with "I think" or "maybe" on things we know. If we don't know, say "let me check and come back to you."
- Don't pitch a different course they didn't ask about.
- Don't end with "Let me know if you have any other questions" alone — pair it with a concrete next step.

## Reference reply

> Subject: Re: [their subject]
>
> Hi [FirstName],
>
> Good to hear from you — and thanks for following up on [thing they referenced].
>
> [Direct answer to their question, two to three sentences. If it's a resource, name it and how they'll get it. If it's about fit, be honest about whether the course suits them.]
>
> [One concrete next step: a date to look out for, a call offer, or a question back. Example: "The next intake opens on [INTAKE DATE] — I'll send the enrolment link the week before." Or: "If it'd help, grab 15 minutes here: [CALENDAR LINK]."]
>
> Best wishes,
> Yasmin

## Drafting checklist (must all pass before showing the draft)
- [ ] Opener is `Hi [FirstName],` — not "Hello", not "Hey".
- [ ] No "Thanks so much for getting in touch" or similar throat-clearing.
- [ ] Their actual question is answered in the first paragraph.
- [ ] British English (no "favorite", "color", "realize" — use favourite, colour, realise).
- [ ] Contractions used naturally.
- [ ] No exclamation marks.
- [ ] No invented specifics — placeholders for anything we don't know.
- [ ] Ends with one concrete next step (not just "let me know if you have questions").
- [ ] Sign-off is `Best wishes,` then `Yasmin` on a new line.
- [ ] Length: three short paragraphs or fewer.

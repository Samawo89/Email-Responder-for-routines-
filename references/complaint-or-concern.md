# Reference: Complaint / Concern

## When to use this reference
A current student or member is raising an issue with course quality, delivery, value, communication, or experience. They want to be heard and want a fix — but they are NOT asking for a refund. Typical signals:
- "I wanted to flag something..."
- "I've been a bit disappointed by..."
- "Some of us have been talking and..."
- Polite but firm tone. They may name other students.
- They want a conversation or a solution, not their money back.

NOT for:
- Refund requests (use `refund-request.md`)
- Cold partnership pitches that are off-brand (we don't have a polite-decline reference yet — flag it to Yasmin)
- General questions (use `student-member-question.md`)

**If the email is a complaint AND a refund request, classify as `refund-request` — the transactional ask takes priority.**

## Voice and tone rules
- Calm, grown-up, accountable. Not defensive.
- British English. Contractions throughout.
- Acknowledge specifically — name the actual issue back to them, not a vague "your concerns".
- Don't over-apologise. One clean acknowledgement is enough. Repeated "I'm so sorry"s sound weak.
- Don't blame anyone — not a tutor, not another student, not the platform.
- Offer something real. A call, a fix, a change to delivery, a check-in date. Not a free month or a discount as a reflex.

## What to do
- Open by naming the specific concern back to them so they know we read it properly.
- Take ownership in one short sentence. "That's not the experience we want anyone to have" is fine.
- Offer a concrete next step — a 20-minute call is usually the right move for anything significant.
- If we're changing something on our side because of this, say so plainly. Don't promise changes we can't make.
- If they mentioned other students, ask whether they'd like us to reach out to them too, or whether they'd prefer to keep it between us.

## What NOT to do
- Don't say "I'm sorry you feel that way." It's a non-apology and it lands badly.
- Don't get defensive or list everything we do well. Save it for the call.
- Don't promise a refund or discount in this reply — that's not what they asked for and it can come across as buying them off.
- Don't be brief to the point of cold. Two to four short paragraphs is the right shape.
- Don't CC anyone they didn't include.

## Reference reply

> Subject: Re: [their subject]
>
> Hi [FirstName],
>
> Thanks for taking the time to write this — and for being direct about it. You're right that [name the specific issue back, e.g. "the last two live sessions have leaned more theoretical than the brief promised"], and that's not the experience we want anyone in the programme to have.
>
> Here's what I'd like to do. I'd like to jump on a short call this week — 20 minutes, no agenda beyond hearing the full picture from you. From there I can come back with what we're going to change and when. [If relevant: "I'll also speak to [TUTOR NAME] before we talk so I'm not asking you to repeat anything you've already said in session."]
>
> Are any of these any good: [PROPOSE 2-3 SLOTS] — or send a couple that work for you and I'll fit around it.
>
> Best wishes,
> Yasmin

## Drafting checklist (must all pass before showing the draft)
- [ ] Opener is `Hi [FirstName],` — not "Hello", not "Hey".
- [ ] The specific concern is named back in the first paragraph (not "your concerns" — the actual thing).
- [ ] One clean acknowledgement. No repeated apologies.
- [ ] No "I'm sorry you feel that way" or any variant.
- [ ] No defensive listing of what we do well.
- [ ] A concrete next step is offered — almost always a 20-minute call with 2–3 proposed slots.
- [ ] No refund / discount offered (this is not a refund email).
- [ ] No blame placed on anyone — tutor, student, platform.
- [ ] British English. Contractions throughout. No exclamation marks.
- [ ] Sign-off is `Best wishes,` then `Yasmin` on a new line.
- [ ] Length: two to four short paragraphs.

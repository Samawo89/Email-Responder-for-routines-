# Reference: Refund Request

## When to use this reference
Someone is explicitly asking for a refund, cancellation, or withdrawal from the programme. Distinct from a complaint — they want their money back, not just a fix. Typical signals:
- "I'd like to request a refund..."
- "I want to cancel my enrolment..."
- "Can I withdraw from the course and get my deposit back?"
- A complaint that ends with "...so I'd like a full refund please."

**Use this reference even if the email also contains complaint content. Refund classification takes priority over complaint classification.**

## Voice and tone rules
- Calm, respectful, completely non-defensive. Treat them like an adult making a clear-headed decision.
- British English. Contractions throughout.
- Don't try to talk them out of it in this reply. The reply's job is to acknowledge cleanly and propose a short call.
- Don't promise the refund or refuse it in writing. The decision happens on the call, against the actual policy — not in an email reply.
- Don't apologise five times. Once, clean, then move on.
- This is the highest-stakes reply we write. Read it twice before showing it to Yasmin.

## What to do
- Acknowledge the request directly in the first sentence — no euphemisms.
- Briefly reflect the situation back so they know we read it properly (one sentence is enough).
- Propose a 20-minute call this week. Frame it as "so I can talk you through how we handle this" — not "so I can change your mind".
- Mention the policy exists and we'll apply it fairly, without quoting specifics in the email.
- If they've named a specific reason (e.g. theory-heavy delivery, missed sessions, life change), reference it back so they feel heard.

## What NOT to do
- Don't say "I'm sorry to hear that" — too soft for the moment. Acknowledge directly instead.
- Don't quote policy in the email reply. That conversation is for the call.
- Don't make any offer in writing — no refund amount, no credit, no transfer to a future cohort. Those are call decisions.
- Don't be defensive about the course or list its strengths.
- Don't loop in a tutor by name without checking with Yasmin first.
- Don't promise a timeline ("you'll have the refund by Friday") in writing.

## Reference reply

> Subject: Re: [their subject]
>
> Hi [FirstName],
>
> Thanks for being straightforward with me about this. I've read your email properly — [one-sentence reflection of their situation, e.g. "you've attended the first two sessions, found the delivery heavier on theory than the brief led you to expect, and would like to withdraw."].
>
> I'd like to talk this through with you on a short call before anything else — 20 minutes, so I can hear the full picture and walk you through how we handle this. We've got a process for it and I want to make sure we apply it properly and fairly to you.
>
> Are any of these any good: [PROPOSE 2-3 SLOTS] — or send a couple of times that work for you and I'll fit around it.
>
> Best wishes,
> Yasmin

## Drafting checklist (must all pass before showing the draft)
- [ ] Opener is `Hi [FirstName],` — not "Hello", not "Hey".
- [ ] The refund request is acknowledged directly in the first sentence (not danced around).
- [ ] One specific detail from their email is reflected back.
- [ ] No "I'm sorry to hear that" or any soft non-acknowledgement.
- [ ] No defence of the course / restatement of value.
- [ ] No specific refund offer, no policy quoted, no timeline promised in writing.
- [ ] A 20-minute call is proposed with 2–3 specific slots.
- [ ] No tutor / staff names without `[CHECK WITH YASMIN]` flag.
- [ ] British English. Contractions throughout. No exclamation marks.
- [ ] Sign-off is `Best wishes,` then `Yasmin` on a new line.
- [ ] Length: three short paragraphs. Tight.
- [ ] **Read twice before showing to Yasmin** — this is the highest-stakes reply in the bank.
